# Game Stages and Common Mistakes

The cleanest way to think about early, mid, and late game in Dune is not by turn number alone. Think by **political state**:

- Before serious alliance structure.
- During alliance consolidation.
- After the table can count reachable win paths.

## Stage priorities

| Stage | Priorities |
|---|---|
| Early | Protect your mobility anchor, buy or threaten useful cards, avoid vanity battles, map likely spice and ally lanes |
| Mid | Convert hand quality into selective fights, form the alliance that fixes your bottleneck, punish overextension, block easy thirds |
| Late | Count every reachable stronghold, value denial over savings, respect Guild and Fremen end states, and ask who still moves after you |

## Early game

Focus on:

- Spice collection.
- Getting useful treachery cards.
- Avoiding costly fights.
- Understanding player intentions.
- Forming temporary non-aggression deals.
- Protecting a mobility city if your faction depends on one.
- Mapping likely ally lanes.

Do not rush strongholds unless the opening is genuinely cheap.

## Mid game

Focus on:

- Positioning near strongholds.
- Identifying the leading player or alliance.
- Building a credible threat.
- Preserving enough troops for a final push.
- Forcing opponents to spend resources.
- Converting hand quality into selective fights.
- Forming an alliance that solves your current bottleneck.

This is where the political game intensifies.

## Late game

Focus on:

- Exact stronghold counts.
- Who moves before whom.
- Whether an alliance can win this turn.
- Who can block whom.
- Whether you can win now.
- Whether you need to stop someone else before pursuing your own plan.
- Whether Guild or Fremen endgame pressure is being underestimated.

Late-game Dune is often about preventing someone else's win before executing your own.

## Sample opening template

For a standard six-player basic game:

1. **Turn one:** preserve or contest a mobility city only if doing so does not cripple your treasury.
2. **First bidding round:** buy for impact, not for hand fullness; a premium weapon and defense pair is better than random bulk.
3. **First shipment:** prefer strongholds or safe harvest setups over empty-desert theatrics; the basic shipping tariff makes non-stronghold deployment expensive.
4. **First battle you choose:** do not make it a fair test of forces; make it an information test, a denial fight, or a card-quality fight.

## Late-game checklist

- Have I counted **all** players who can still reach a needed stronghold after me?
- If I take this stronghold, can I survive not one battle, but the full remainder of the round?
- If I cannot win, what is the cheapest move that **prevents** the current leader from winning?
- Am I underestimating Guild's end-game pressure or Fremen's alternative finish?
- Am I risking a top leader in a battle that a cheap hero or weaker leader could test instead?
- Is my spice better spent on denial shipping now than on hypothetical value later?

## Common beginner mistakes

| Mistake | Why it is bad |
|---|---|
| Fighting too much | Burns troops and cards for little gain |
| Ignoring spice | Leaves you unable to act |
| Sitting on strongholds too early | Makes you the table target |
| Forgetting traitors | Can lose key leaders instantly |
| Bidding too aggressively | Leaves you poor and predictable |
| Never negotiating | Gives up one of the main tools of the game |
| Trusting alliances blindly | Partners may have better timing than you |
| Ignoring storm and turn order | Good positions become dead positions |
| Overcommitting in combat | Winning one battle can cost the war |
| Landing on CHOAM charity unnecessarily | Signals that your threats are limited |
| Treating alliances as friendships | Alliances are tactical mergers, not promises of equal benefit |
| Exposing crucial leaders in low-value fights | Gives traitors and card packages too much leverage |

## Final practical advice

Do not try to play perfectly. Dune is too political and too asymmetric for clean optimization.

Aim to be:

- Hard to attack profitably.
- Useful enough that others deal with you.
- Dangerous enough that others respect you.
- Patient enough not to waste your army.
- Alert enough to recognize the winning turn.

The player who wins Dune is often not the player who looked strongest all game. It is the player who preserved enough resources, information, and leverage to strike when everyone else was slightly too weak to stop them.
