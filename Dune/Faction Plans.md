# Faction Plans

## Summary table

| Faction | Core abilities and identity | Main strengths | Main weaknesses | Recommended playstyle |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Atreides | Bidding prescience, top spice-deck peek before movement, one-element battle peek, early Arrakeen start, movement-3 access if city held | Information economy, strong positional flexibility, high-quality battle decisions | Spice-poor relative to needs; must both buy cards and ship forces | **Defensive-information broker early; precise striker midgame; opportunist finisher late** |
| Harkonnen | Keep all drawn traitors, hand limit 8, buy one get one card, Carthag start | Threat density, bluff pressure, strong opening volatility | Income can collapse if overbidding or overfighting | **Front-load pressure, buy aggressively but not ruinously, pick fights that force bad leader/card usage** |
| Emperor | Gets other players' bidding payments, large opening bankroll | Stable economy, can underwrite alliance plans, strong city play | Too easy to become rich and passive; visible board targets | **Convert money into board leverage, not hoarding; hold at least one mobility city and fund decisive shipments** |
| Spacing Guild | Paid for others' shipments, flexible shipping types, cheap shipping, special default win if nobody wins by game end | Tempo control, map-wide repositioning, best denial faction | Weak leaders, slower card timing early, can look weak before round timing matters | **Play spoiler-controller first, kingmaker second, default-winner always** |
| Fremen | Free deployment near Great Flat, 2-space movement, worm-riding, alternative endgame win if the board stalls | Best natural desert mobility, low cash deployment cost, denial power in long games | Low starting spice, poor early bidding position, prone to under-equipment in base play | **Patience first, board spread second, selective strike and denial finish** |
| Bene Gesserit | Prediction, free shadow shipping into Polar Sink when others arrive from orbit, Voice in battle | Table influence, battle control, political leverage, alliance desirability | Slow revival, can become too passive; in pure basic game less board-evasive than advanced reputation suggests | **Manipulate pace, avoid fair attrition, win via timing, alliance leverage, or prediction pressure** |

## Atreides

You win through information.

Your early game is about **preserving Arrakeen and monetizing information without fully commoditizing it**. Your income comes from the planet, and your movement-3 advantage matters. Defend your city, bid selectively on high-impact cards, use spice-deck information to choose favorable harvest races, and sell battle information in a way that keeps everyone dependent on you rather than fully informed.

Midgame, fight only when prescience or card tracking lets you compress losses.

Late game, Atreides wins by appearing disciplined until the round where discipline becomes a sudden multi-stronghold jump.

Basic plan:

- Gather information.
- Buy selectively.
- Sell information without giving away the whole table.
- Form targeted deals.
- Fight only when your information converts into a real edge.
- Punish overconfident opponents.

Atreides often wants an ally who supplies money, battle compression, or a reliable finishing threat.

## Harkonnen

You win through treachery-card volume, intimidation, and traitor pressure.

Harkonnen is strongest **early**, when fresh traitor uncertainty and double-card acquisition make the whole table afraid of you. But fear does not pay shipping bills.

Your plan is not "fight everything." It is:

> Fight the battles that strip hands, test leader safety, or produce captures.

Bid above market for genuinely important cards, but never so high that you land on CHOAM charity. Midgame Harkonnen often wins less through revealed traitors than through the **threat** of traitors, which pins opposing good leaders.

Late game, keep pressure asymmetric. Punish overextended factions and avoid equal-value slugfests you cannot finance twice.

Basic plan:

- Build a dangerous hand.
- Make opponents afraid to use their best leaders.
- Bid aggressively, but not ruinously.
- Exploit uncertainty around traitors.
- Pick fights that strip hands, test leaders, or create stronghold pressure.

## Emperor

You win through money.

Other players' bids enrich you, so you often grow stronger as the table arms itself. The danger is assuming that rich means safe.

Shipping everything in early can create board exposure before you are ready. The better plan is to convert bidding income into **board tempo**:

- Stabilize one city.
- Keep enough spice to answer a sudden alliance window.
- Become the partner who can turn someone else's plan from "almost" into "now."

Midgame Emperor is often strongest as a backer of decisive movement or game-saving blocks.

Late game, if you are rich but not dictating cities and deployments, you are probably losing despite your treasury.

Basic plan:

- Accumulate spice.
- Convert money into board leverage.
- Hold or contest a mobility city when practical.
- Fund decisive shipments.
- Use superior economy to take or defend strongholds at the right moment.

## Spacing Guild

You win through mobility, patience, and denial.

Guild is the classic faction that feels weak when played flatly and oppressive when played well. Its mobility and cheap shipping become strongest once resources build. A lost-looking long game can be converted by weakening likely winners until the round ends.

Early game Guild should avoid unnecessary combat, accumulate shipment money, and threaten selective presence rather than broad presence.

Midgame, you are the best **blocker** at the table. One troop in the right stronghold can force an entire round to re-route.

Late game, your special win condition is not just a tiebreaker. It is negotiating leverage.

Ask every player, every late round:

> How much is my non-interference worth to you?

Basic plan:

- Stay flexible.
- Avoid unnecessary losses.
- Profit from shipping.
- Tax the table politically.
- Use small deployments to block win paths.
- Preserve the threat of the default win condition in long games.

## Fremen

You win through planet control and endurance.

Fremen often look weaker to beginners because they are not as dominant in bidding or shipping as Emperor or Guild. That is misleading in long games.

In the basic game, Fremen's problem is not movement. It is **equipment timing**. You are poor, can rarely buy cards early, and should wait for accessible spice blows while avoiding premature battles.

Use your deployment freedom and 2-space movement to occupy neglected geometry, not to launch fair early fights. Midgame, your best rounds are the rounds after other factions have already spent spice and cards hurting each other.

Late game, keep the special victory condition in mind earlier than most players do. The table often remembers it one turn too late.

Advanced rules materially improve Fremen's combat economics. In pure basic, be more restrained.

Basic plan:

- Use native movement and deployment advantages.
- Build quietly.
- Preserve forces.
- Avoid fair early fights.
- Become harder to remove over time.
- Keep alternative win conditions in mind if your ruleset uses them.

## Bene Gesserit

You win through manipulation, prediction, battle control, and timing.

The strongest beginner rule is:

> Do not let your low revival rate drag you into normal attrition.

In the basic game, your free shadow-shipping goes into **Polar Sink**, which gives central reach but does not equal the full advanced-game advisor and coexistence toolkit many experienced players associate with the faction.

Your board game is quieter in basic, but your battle leverage is still elite because Voice can erase key card lines.

Early game: build a hand and remain hard to read.

Midgame: intervene when a win must be denied or when your opponent is exposed to Voice-based punishment.

Late game: weaponize prediction pressure even when the prediction itself is not live. The threat changes behavior.

Bludgeon's phrasing that Bene Gesserit combines strong combat ability with an ability to avoid combat captures the spirit, but in pure basic the "avoid combat" side is more about timing and deterrence than advanced coexistence tricks.

Basic plan:

- Avoid normal attrition.
- Build a useful hand.
- Use Voice to distort battle incentives.
- Intervene when a win must be denied.
- Keep prediction pressure alive.
- Use alliances to turn timing control into a finishing threat.
